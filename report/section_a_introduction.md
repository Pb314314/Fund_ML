## Section A: Introduction

### A.1 The AI Divide as Global Risk

LLM capabilities remain concentrated in a handful of economies despite widespread open-weight releases. As of 2025, the United States controls approximately $74\%$ of global high-end AI compute capacity, China holds $14\%$, and the European Union retains merely $4.8\%$ [1]—[2]. This asymmetry leaves 160 nations as "compute deserts," dependent on foreign cloud infrastructure [3]. The consequences are severe: only $5\%$ of African AI researchers possess adequate compute access, $95\%$ rely on free-tier tools such as Google Colab, and the continent hosts zero top-tier supercomputers [2]—[3].

Policy responses align with compute concentration, raising distributional concerns. UNESCO's 2021 Recommendation on the Ethics of AI, adopted by 194 member states, warns that AI can deepen divides and assigns solidarity obligations to advanced nations [5]. The EU AI Act (August 2024) establishes risk-tiered obligations for high-risk systems—including bias-free training data and human oversight—but its scope centers on EU deployment rather than global equity [6]. The U.S. CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 appropriated $\$52.7$ billion to reshore semiconductor manufacturing, with $\$39$ billion for domestic fabrication incentives and prohibitions on expansion in countries of concern [7]. We define the **AI Divide** as the systemic concentration of AI benefits, governance authority, and technical capacity within wealthy nations, compounded by barriers that prevent equitable participation in model development and deployment.

### A.2 Scope and Objectives

This paper investigates how compute and data disparities create systemic LLM inference performance gaps across the Global South. We quantify the **tokenization tax**—wherein low-resource languages incur $2$–$5\times$ more tokens per word than English under standard byte-pair encoding—and the **memory wall**, wherein key-value cache growth on constrained hardware forces truncated context windows [4]. Africa, home to over $2{,}000$ languages, exemplifies data scarcity: only ~$42$ languages receive LLM support, and Swahili—spoken by $200$ million people—claims merely $700$ million tokens in existing corpora [4].

Our analysis adopts a dual-viewpoint lens: the structural-governance perspective examines how export controls, subsidy regimes, and risk-based regulations shape access; the market-self-correction perspective evaluates whether open-weight models, cloud API commoditization, and community adaptation can narrow gaps without centralized intervention.

### A.3 Contribution Statement

The primary contributions are threefold. First, we empirically quantify compounding resource inequity by combining real tokenizer fertility measurements with memory-bandwidth simulations across hardware tiers—from NVIDIA H100 data-center GPUs to consumer-grade RTX 4060 cards. Second, we introduce a reproducible analytical framework linking hardware specifications, tokenization efficiency, and end-to-end inference latency, enabling analysts to estimate performance gaps for underrepresented languages. Third, we offer a structured dual-viewpoint evaluation of governance mechanisms versus market adaptation, identifying the conditions under which each approach mitigates or exacerbates the AI Divide.
