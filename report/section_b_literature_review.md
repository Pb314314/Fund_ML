## Section B: Literature Review

### B.1 Geopolitics of Compute

The geographic distribution of AI infrastructure critically determines national AI capacity. The Governance of AI Program at Oxford argues that compute is a uniquely effective governance lever because—unlike data or algorithms—it is excludable and detectable, enabling targeted regulatory intervention [1]. This concentration carries equity implications: Lehdonvirta et al. observe that nations lacking territorial access to advanced semiconductor clusters forfeit "computational sovereignty," rendering them structurally dependent on dominant regions [2]. The Tony Blair Institute confirms that compute access in the Global South remains constrained by supply-chain bottlenecks and pricing premiums [3]. Policy responses have accelerated bifurcation. The U.S. CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 allocated $52.7 billion in subsidies while prohibiting recipients from expanding advanced manufacturing capacity in China for ten years [7]. Complementing these measures, successive U.S. export control regimes have banned NVIDIA H100 and H200 accelerators outright and restricted the H20 variant, tightening frontier hardware supply to non-allied nations [7][4].

### B.2 Linguistic Inequity: The Tokenization Tax

Even when hardware is available, multilingual deployment faces efficiency penalties from subword tokenization. Lundin et al. quantify this "tokenization tax," demonstrating that each additional token per target-language word relative to English correlates with an 8–18 percentage point accuracy degradation on downstream tasks [5]. The penalty is regressive: African languages incur premiums of 2–5× versus English, and because transformer attention scales quadratically with sequence length, excess tokens inflate latency and memory costs superlinearly [5][6]. Ahuja et al. propose cost-performance optimization for low-resource settings, yet their solutions assume token budgets unaffordable in the regions most affected [6]. Recent vocabulary compression work offers partial relief but does not eliminate the fundamental asymmetry [7].

### B.3 Data Imbalance and Model Bias

Training data availability compounds linguistic inequity with geographic imbalance. Joshi et al. and the Masakhane collective document over 2,000 African languages, of which approximately 42 receive any LLM support and only 52 have viable machine-translation systems [14][15]. The gap is stark: Swahili, with over 200 million speakers, contributes only ~700 million tokens to pretraining corpora [15]. Bender et al.'s "Stochastic Parrots" critique demonstrates that training data encodes societal biases, overrepresenting digitally privileged demographics while marginalizing underrepresented populations [13]. The African Languages Lab has compiled resources spanning 40 languages totaling 19 billion tokens—meaningful, yet paling beside English-centric collections [15].

### B.4 The Memory Wall

Inference scalability is constrained not by arithmetic throughput alone but by memory bandwidth. Hooper et al. introduce KVQuant, a quantization framework for key-value caches enabling context lengths approaching 10 million tokens [9]. CentroidKV compresses cache state through centroid-based clustering for long-sequence inference [8], while Wang et al. develop look-once strategies to reduce redundant KV cache operations [10]. These algorithmic advances, however, confront a stark hardware reality: high-bandwidth memory (HBM3e) achieves 3.35 TB/s, whereas commodity GDDR6 peaks at 272 GB/s—a 12× bandwidth differential that stratifies which deployments sustain long-context inference [12]. The ITU's computational efficiency metrics underscore that this memory wall widens the capability gap between hyperscaler datacenters and resource-constrained environments [12].

Collectively, this literature establishes that the Global AI Divide is not a transient market artifact but a structurally reinforced phenomenon operating across hardware geography, linguistic representation, data availability, and memory architecture. The sections that follow translate these mechanisms into empirical measurements.
